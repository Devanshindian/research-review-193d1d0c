Summary

'''State of Skills-Based Hiring: Annual Benchmark Report''' is an annual benchmark report on the adoption, methods, outcomes, and risks of skills-based hiring, an approach that evaluates candidates against job-relevant abilities and competencies rather than relying chiefly on degrees, job titles, or employer pedigree.12 It examines how employers define role requirements, use structured interviews and work-sample assessments, and measure hiring outcomes such as quality of hire, retention, cost, and candidate experience.34

The report situates skills-based hiring within broader efforts to ease labor-market skills shortages and reduce unnecessary degree requirements. Surveys cited in the report found that 70% of employers used skills-based hiring for entry-level roles in 2025, while adoption and implementation varied substantially by industry and hiring stage.5 It also distinguishes policy commitments and degree-free job postings from demonstrated changes in hiring outcomes: a 2024 analysis estimated that fewer than one in 700 U.S. hires in 2023 directly benefited from employers removing degree requirements.6

The report emphasizes that skills-based hiring is not inherently equitable or predictive without job-related assessment design and ongoing evaluation. It highlights structured interviews and multiple assessment measures as alternatives to unstructured selection, while addressing adverse-impact rules, recordkeeping, candidate transparency, and the potential for automated screening systems to reproduce demographic bias.37

Background

Skills-based hiring assesses candidates according to demonstrated ability to perform a role’s essential functions, including specific technical and soft skills, rather than treating degrees, job titles, employer pedigree, or years of experience as primary proxies for competence.189 It identifies the knowledge, abilities, and competencies needed for an open role, then evaluates whether a candidate matches those requirements.2 Traditional hiring instead commonly foregrounds educational attainment, prior experience, background, college attended, degree earned, and job titles, even though those signals may not reliably show whether a person can perform the actual work.10

The model has become more prevalent as employers confront skilled-worker shortages, rapidly changing job roles, and the limits of resumes as hiring signals.11 As of early 2026, 70% of employers reported using skills-based hiring for entry-level roles, up from 65% the previous year.12 A separate employer survey found that 64.8% use skills-based practices for new entry-level hires, with more than half using them always or most of the time.10 TestGorilla’s 2024 State of Skills-Based Hiring reported that 81% of employers globally use some form of skills-based hiring, while 95% agreed it is the dominant recruitment method of the future; marketing had 95% adoption in that survey.13 Another source reports that 85% of employers use skills-based practices and that approximately 64.8% apply them in entry-level hiring.9

Adoption is not limited to one point in the process. Among employers that use skills-based hiring, 90% report using it during interviews and two-thirds use it in screening.10 Nearly three-quarters of employers in the same study had developed competency-based job descriptions.10 Employers may operationalize the approach through skills-based screening before resume review, multi-measure testing, structured interviews, work samples, and job-relevant skills tests.313 A skills-based system requires the same capability definitions to guide intake discussions, screening logic, interview structure, and performance-feedback loops so that evaluation signals stay aligned from initial screening through offer.9

Skills-first and skills-based are related but distinct terms. “Skills-first” is presented as a broader organizational stance, while skills-based hiring is the practical process of assessing candidates against a shared role-specific rubric.12 Simply changing job descriptions is insufficient: the process must also change across sourcing, screening, interviewing, and final selection.12 The recommended sequence is to pilot a few measurable roles, map the competencies of top performers, train hiring teams, and update job descriptions last.12 Consistency matters because every stakeholder, from sourcer through final interviewer, must assess candidates against the same rubric.12

The shift also reflects growing scrutiny of degree requirements. In 2024, about 45% of organizations reportedly stopped requiring a bachelor’s degree for some jobs, in addition to the 55% that had already done so in 2023.11 In a poll of 800 U.S. employers, about 80% said they would prefer a person with relevant experience to a college graduate when hiring.11 Named employers reported as having removed degree requirements include IBM, Google, Delta Air Lines, and Bank of America.11 Skills-first systems can recognize capability gained through work experience, apprenticeships, certifications, or self-study rather than only through formal education.14

The case for the model is both operational and equity-related. Removing outdated degree requirements, arbitrary experience thresholds, and screening steps that add time without improving quality can widen the talent pool and reduce hiring bottlenecks.12 Skills-based hiring is described as creating speed by expanding nontraditional sourcing and removing process bottlenecks that add days to each hire.12 Time-to-fill and retention are identified as central outcome metrics, although they become meaningful only after the process is applied consistently.12 One source reports that 75% of employers using skills-based assessment tools saw improved quality of hire and that adopting organizations tend to see approximately 30% higher retention within two years of hire.9

Skills-based hiring can also reduce reliance on credentials that may restrict access to workers Skilled Through Alternative Routes, or STARs.15 Public-sector employers can draw on Opportunity @ Work’s STARs Public Sector Hub, which provides data, narratives, support, best practices, and a community for government leaders; Business Roundtable’s Multiple Pathways Initiative offers example company actions, guides, principles, success stories, and working groups on skills-based talent practices.15 Equity requires attention beyond hiring access: employers should avoid creating a career ceiling for workers without degrees after they are hired.15

A defensible skills-based process must be designed for fairness, not merely for higher-volume screening. Structured interviews, defined as asking every candidate the same questions and using the same scoring rubric with multiple raters, had mean predictive validity of r = .42 in Sackett, Zhang, Berry and Lievens’ 2023 meta-analysis update, the highest validity reported among common predictors, while producing substantially smaller demographic gaps than cognitive-ability tests.3 Unstructured interviews are described as having both lower predictive validity and greater demographic disparity; standardized scorecards with behavioral anchors for each rating level are identified as a direct method for reducing that gap.3

No single assessment should function as the sole gate. Combining a structured interview, work sample, and job-relevant skills test can reduce the demographic gap associated with any one method while improving overall validity.3 TestGorilla’s 2024 survey reported that 91% of employers using two or more measurement methods were satisfied.3 Other evidence-backed practices for reducing adverse impact include validating that selection methods are job-related, monitoring the four-fifths rule on an ongoing basis, auditing AI tools, and anonymizing resume review.3

Legal and compliance requirements remain central. The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission’s Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures were adopted on August 25, 1978 by the EEOC, Office of Personnel Management, Department of Justice, Treasury Department, and Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs; supplemental questions 91 through 93 took effect on May 2, 1980.16 The Guidelines are intended to support equal employment opportunity without discrimination.16 Where the total selection process for a job has adverse impact, individual components should be evaluated for adverse impact; where the total process does not have adverse impact, federal enforcement agencies generally will not expect component-level adverse-impact evaluation or validation in ordinary circumstances.7 Records should be safeguarded and used appropriately for adverse-impact analysis and, where required, affirmative-action program development and monitoring.7

Background checks can undermine equitable access when criminal-history filters are overly broad, because they disproportionately exclude Black and Hispanic applicants.3 EEOC guidance requires individualized assessment and job-relatedness for any disqualifying arrest or conviction record; adjudication matrices can embed this approach in the background-check workflow.3 Compensation practices also matter: pay-history requests, low-anchored offers, and inflexible negotiation policies can create disparities that persist in later compensation studies.3

Algorithmic hiring increases the need for measurement. When an AI filter touches every application, even a small per-applicant bias can compound into a large aggregate disparity.3 Skills-based hiring programs should therefore document criteria, collect and analyze outcomes data, and provide transparent evaluations across agencies or business units to sustain support and identify inequitable results.2

Methodology

Defining skills-based hiring

Skills-based hiring identifies the specific knowledge, abilities, and competencies a candidate possesses and evaluates whether they match the requirements of an open role. It shifts evaluation away from proxies such as colleges attended, degrees earned, and prior job titles.2

The methodology should begin with the work itself: define the outcomes, tasks, knowledge, technical skills, and transferable skills required for each role. A skills-based job posting should describe action-oriented responsibilities, use clear and accessible language, provide a realistic preview of daily work, and replace years-of-experience requirements with performance expectations.14

Competency-model development

Build competency models from observed performance in the role. Start with current top performers, identify the competencies they share, and compare top, middle, and lower performers to determine which skills distinguish strong hires from average hires.12

Keep competency models simple enough for hiring managers and interview teams who are not HR specialists to apply consistently; overly complex models with 10 or 12 criteria can reduce usability.12 Consult hiring managers and operators who work with the role daily, and account for how the role is likely to change over the next 18 months, including AI-driven changes in work.12

Each model should balance technical proficiency with soft skills such as communication, problem-solving, and the ability to manage difficulty, because these capabilities can distinguish a good hire from a great one.12

Evidence collection across the hiring funnel

Measure the full selection process rather than only the final decision. The U.S. Uniform Guidelines require employers to maintain records that disclose the impact of tests and other selection procedures on employment opportunities by identifiable race, sex, or ethnic group; where applicant volumes are large and procedures are frequent, appropriate and adequately sized samples may be retained.7

Record selection rates by sex and by the specified race and ethnicity categories, including Black, American Indian or Alaska Native, Asian or Pacific Islander, Hispanic, non-Hispanic white, and total applicants.7 If the total selection process shows adverse impact, evaluate its individual components for adverse impact as well.7

Track sourcing separately because the initial candidate pool constrains downstream selection rates. Single-network, university-list, or referral-chain sourcing can narrow demographic representation before screening begins; multi-source sourcing broadens the pool.3

Track outcomes at each stage, including application, screening, assessment, phone screen, interview, offer, and withdrawal. In Starred’s 2025 candidate-experience data, 69.49% of rejected candidates were rejected at the application stage, up from 62.4% in 2024, and nearly 90% of survey invitations were sent to rejected candidates.17

Assessing candidate capability

Use job-relevant, practical assessments that allow candidates to demonstrate work capability. For a data analyst role, an assessment may ask candidates to analyze a dataset, build a visualization, and write a short summary of insights, rather than infer SQL, dashboard, or data-interpretation ability from a degree and résumé.4

Use multiple measures rather than a single gate. Combining a structured interview, work sample, and job-relevant skills test can reduce the demographic gap associated with any one method while improving overall validity.3 TestGorilla’s 2024 State of Skills-Based Hiring survey found that 91% of employers using two or more measurement methods were satisfied with their hiring process.3

Structured interviews should use the same questions, the same scoring rubric, and multiple raters.3 Sackett, Zhang, Berry, and Lievens’ 2023 predictive-validity meta-analysis reported mean validity of r = .42 for structured interviews, the highest of common predictors, with substantially smaller demographic gaps than cognitive-ability tests.3

Behavioral interview questions can be structured and evaluated with the STAR framework: Situation, Task, Action, and Result. The method prompts candidates to give concrete examples and helps interviewers assess job-related competencies rather than vague impressions.18 Scorecards should define strong, average, and weak answers for every question to support consistent evaluation across interviewers.18

Adverse-impact monitoring and validation

Apply the four-fifths rule to selection rates. Under 29 CFR § 1607.4, a selection rate for any race, sex, or ethnic group that is less than four-fifths, or 80%, of the rate for the group with the highest selection rate is generally regarded as evidence of adverse impact.7

Adverse impact is an outcome measure rather than a measure of intent: a test, screening rubric, interview format, or algorithm can create disparity even when decision-makers acted in good faith.3 Disparate impact requires no proof of discriminatory intent; the Supreme Court established the theory in *Griggs v. Duke Power Co.*, 401 U.S. 424 (1971), and Congress codified it in the Civil Rights Act of 1991.3

Monitor each selection component when the overall process shows adverse impact and in circumstances identified in the Uniform Guidelines, including where a procedure perpetuates assignment patterns caused by prior discriminatory practices or where courts or administrative interpretations have found a procedure not job-related in similar circumstances.7

Use structured interviews, multi-measure assessments, job-relatedness validation, ongoing four-fifths monitoring, skills-based hiring, algorithmic audits, and anonymized résumé review as evidence-backed adverse-impact mitigations.3

AI and automated-selection controls

Measure AI use, purpose, and oversight separately. SHRM’s 2025 Talent Trends report, based on n = 2,040, found that 43% of organizations used AI in HR tasks, up from 26% in 2024; 44% of organizations using AI for recruiting used it to screen résumés.3

Audit automated screening for disparate outcomes. A University of Washington audit published in October 2024 compared more than 3 million résumés against more than 550 real job listings and found that three large language models preferred white-associated names 85% of the time, compared with 9% for Black-associated names; Black male-associated names were never preferred over white male names in the sample.3

Document whether demographic characteristics are removed before AI scoring, which data sources feed matching or ranking systems, and whether models are periodically tested for adverse impact.3 Video and behavioral-AI assessments also require transparency because systems may analyze facial expressions, speech patterns, and communication style, while privacy and ethical concerns and emerging European and U.S. state requirements are increasing scrutiny of algorithmic scoring.19

Candidate-experience measurement

Evaluate candidate experience across attraction, execution, and selection. The Candidate Experience Driver Framework defines attraction through job and company information, employer brand, and hiring-team interactions; execution through application accessibility, expectation-setting, and perceived process duration; and selection through assessment relevance and engagement, opportunity to perform, and transparency of decision criteria.17

Collect feedback from rejected candidates as well as hires, particularly where rejection occurs before meaningful human interaction. Candidates rejected early may lack context about the process, criteria, or differentiating factors and can form negative impressions when those elements are not communicated.17

Assessments should be short, mobile-first, and transparent. Processes that feel like high-stakes final exams can increase candidate drop-off, while candidate experience affects employer brand and the ability to maintain future talent pools.20

Outcome measures

Benchmark implementation against hiring cost, retention, performance, and mis-hire outcomes.

- 74% reporting lower hiring costs.4

- Estimated U.S. employer savings of $7,800 to $22,500 per role through reduced mis-hires.4

- 89% reporting increased employee retention.4

- 92% reporting that skills-based hires outperform hires selected mainly for education or experience.4

- 88% reporting reduced mis-hires.4

Connect assessment and selection data to quality of hire, retention, internal mobility, and succession planning where possible; this distinguishes a one-time test from a predictive hiring system used for ongoing workforce decisions.20

Use transparent outcome evaluation and cross-agency or cross-unit measurement to maintain momentum and support for skills-based strategies.2

Findings

Skills-based hiring adoption and labor-market pressure

NACE's Job Outlook 2026 survey of 183 employers, conducted August 7 to September 22, 2025, found that 70% used skills-based hiring, up from 65% the previous year; 71% said they used it at least half the time.5

TestGorilla reported that 67% of employers still used resumes as a screening tool in 2025, down from 73% in 2024, an 8% year-over-year reduction in reliance on traditional hiring documents.5

The World Economic Forum's *Future of Jobs Report 2025* found that 60% of businesses identified local skills gaps as their largest barrier to business transformation, while only 39% held a positive five-year outlook for talent availability.5

Hiring by explicit skills rather than previous job titles increases the eligible candidate pool.5 SaaS technology and pre-employment testing have reduced the historical cost and difficulty of determining which skills candidates have and how proficient they are, weakening the rationale for using degrees as a proxy for job capability.21

Degree requirements are declining, but the realized effect remains limited

The share of job postings requiring a college degree rose to 19.8% in April 2022, then declined again. The decline persisted after adjusting for changes in job-title mix, indicating that employers increasingly sought workers with less formal education for open roles.21

Between 2020 and 2022, degree requirements increased in 19 of the 47 occupational groups analyzed by Indeed. The largest increases were concentrated in knowledge-work occupations, including physicians and surgeons, architecture, and accounting.21

Accounting postings requiring a college degree increased by 3.4 percentage points between 2020 and 2022, then fell by 8.0 percentage points over the following two years.21

Burning Glass Institute and Harvard Business School's 2024 analysis of more than 11,300 job postings estimated that only 1 in 700 hires in 2023 directly benefited from employers dropping degree requirements. That represented approximately 97,000 workers out of 77 million annual hires.5

Degree requirements remain more likely to persist where postsecondary knowledge and credentials are central to the work, including healthcare and engineering.21 For jobs where a degree is not necessary, requiring one can disadvantage lower-income applicants and create adverse-impact risk.22

Adoption differs sharply by industry

TestGorilla survey data cited in 2025 skills-based-hiring reporting placed 2024 adoption at 95% in marketing, 89% in scientific and technical services, and 89% in construction.5

The lowest reported adoption rates were 54% in hotel and food services, 56% in wholesale, and 61% in government and public institutions.5

CompTIA workforce-survey data cited in the same reporting found that 78% of technology companies had implemented skills-based hiring for technical roles. Those companies reported a 45% increase in candidate diversity and a 35% improvement in retention.5

Assessment design determines whether skills-first hiring improves selection quality

Structured interviews ask every candidate the same predetermined, job-related questions and apply the same standardized scoring criteria.18 The approach requires subject-matter experts to identify role-specific knowledge, skills, abilities, and other characteristics; standardize questions; create scoring rubrics; and train interviewers.18

Structured interviews commonly use behavioral questions about past experience and situational questions about how a candidate would respond to a job-relevant future scenario.18 Google, the U.S. Office of Personnel Management, and the Department of Veterans Affairs have adopted or recommended structured interviewing practices.18

Sackett, Zhang, Berry, and Lievens' 2023 predictive-validity meta-analysis reported mean validity of r = .42 for structured interviews, the highest value among common predictors, while finding substantially smaller demographic gaps than cognitive-ability tests.3 Other meta-analyses cited in the collected research, including Sackett's 2022 analysis and Schmidt and Hunter's 1998 analysis, also ranked structured interviews highly for predicting job success; the collected evidence describes them as nearly twice as predictive as unstructured interviews.18

Unstructured interviews rely more heavily on subjective impressions and can magnify confirmation bias, halo and horn effects, similarity or affinity bias, and first-impression bias.18 Standardized behavioral anchors and interview scorecards address this by applying the same scoring standard to each candidate.3

Employers should not treat a single screen as the sole gate. Combining a structured interview, work sample, and job-relevant skills test can reduce the demographic gap associated with any one method while improving overall validity.3 TestGorilla's 2024 *State of Skills-Based Hiring* survey found that 91% of employers using two or more measurement methods were satisfied with their hiring process.3

Criteria Corp's 2024/2025 Hiring Benchmark Report, based on more than 425 hiring professionals, likewise found greater benefits when employers used multi-measure skills testing alongside behavioral and cognitive assessments than when they used one assessment type.5 Yet SHRM 2024 data found that 62% of HR professionals still considered skill validation difficult because employers often needed additional assessments or certifications to verify claimed competencies.5

Candidate support for skills assessment also increased: 68% of employees preferred hiring processes that included skills-based assessments in 2024, up from 56% in 2023; 84% said such assessments reduced conscious or unconscious bias, up from 69% in 2023.5 Skills-based hires reported higher job satisfaction, with 38% describing themselves as very happy in their roles.5

Job-relatedness and validation remain essential safeguards

A pre-hire test must be relevant to the job. General cognitive-ability tests can be harder to validate as job-related than hard-skills tests and carry greater adverse-impact risk.22

The U.S. Department of Justice and EEOC challenged the New York City Fire Department's 2002 and 2007 written entrance exams because they disproportionately excluded Black and Hispanic applicants. Courts found that the exams lacked content validity and did not have strong evidence of job relatedness.22

A physical assessment may be permissible for a firefighter when it measures an essential function, such as carrying heavy loads up stairs under timed conditions. A cognitive test with demographic disparities requires strong empirical validation that it predicts job performance, a clear job-related connection, and no equally effective, less discriminatory alternative.22

A bona fide occupational qualification is a narrow legal exception that permits consideration of protected characteristics only when reasonably necessary to normal business operations. The employer bears the burden of proving the exception and showing it rejected less discriminatory alternatives.22

AI and automation create concentrated adverse-impact risk

SHRM's 2025 Talent Trends report, based on 2,040 respondents, found that 43% of organizations used AI in HR tasks, up from 26% in 2024. Among organizations using AI for recruiting, 44% used it to screen resumes.3

The University of Washington's October 2024 audit compared large-language-model resume evaluations across more than 3 million resume comparisons and more than 550 real job listings. The models preferred white-associated names over Black-associated names 85% of the time, while Black-associated names were preferred 9% of the time; Black male-associated names were never preferred over white male names in the sample.3

When an algorithmic filter reviews every application, even a small per-applicant bias can compound into a large aggregate disparity.3 The EEOC received 88,531 new discrimination charges in FY2024, 9.2% more than the previous year, and recovered nearly $700 million for more than 21,000 victims.3

Adverse impact under the EEOC's Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures is “a substantially different rate of selection in hiring, promotion or other employment decision” that disadvantages members of a race, sex, or ethnic group.3 It is an outcome measure and does not require discriminatory intent. Disparate impact is the legal theory associated with the statistical disparity, while disparate treatment requires proof of intentional discrimination.3

The Supreme Court recognized disparate-impact liability in *Griggs v. Duke Power Co.*, 401 U.S. 424 (1971), and Congress codified it in the Civil Rights Act of 1991.3 The collected research identifies structured interviews, multi-measure assessment, job-relatedness validation, ongoing four-fifths-rule monitoring, skills-based hiring, algorithmic audits, and anonymized resume review as evidence-backed practices for reducing adverse impact.3

Interpretation and use

Interpret skills-based hiring as a change in the evidence used to judge fit

Skills-based hiring prioritizes a candidate’s skills, competencies, and relevant work experience over traditional proxies such as degrees, colleges attended, and prior job titles.2 Its purpose is to identify the knowledge, abilities, and competencies required for the open role, rather than infer competence from educational pedigree or title history.2

Traditional degree- and title-based screening has two documented limitations: it can produce poor job matches when credentials are treated as the main indicator of ability, and rigid degree or title requirements can unnecessarily narrow the candidate pool.2 Skills-based approaches can increase process complexity, but the cited evidence associates them with better job matches, lower turnover, greater job satisfaction, career mobility, and a more inclusive workforce.2

A degree requirement is not automatically inappropriate, but it should be connected to the work. Requiring a college degree for an entry-level position where it is not actually necessary may disadvantage lower-income applicants and can create adverse-impact risk.22 Employers should replace generic experience thresholds with performance expectations where possible, describe daily work through realistic, action-oriented responsibilities, and use clear, inclusive, accessible language in postings.14

Treat policy commitments as measurable behavior change, not proof of completed transformation

State and territory governments are prominent adopters of skills-based hiring, often using legislation or executive orders to remove degree requirements and broaden access to public-sector work.2 In the years after such action, 22 of the 25 states and territories that adopted these changes increased their share of job postings without degree requirements faster than states and territories that had not promoted skills-based hiring.2

One analysis estimates that, in states with such commitments, degree requirements in state-government job postings fell by 2.5 percentage points for each additional year of policy exposure.23 The same analysis found increased public awareness of skills-based concepts, including the “paper ceiling,” defined as the bachelor’s-degree analogue of the glass ceiling.23

The potential reach is substantial: if skills-based action within state and territorial governments catalyzed broader public-sector change, up to 22.0 million jobs, or 14.1% of U.S. jobs, could be affected.2 Looking only at public-administration roles, excluding public hospitals, educational institutions, and the military, the estimated reach is 10.4 million jobs, or 6.7% of U.S. jobs.2 Government influence may extend further through contractors and private employment supported by public spending and procurement.2

These figures should be used as indicators that policy commitments can shift posting practices and expand access, not as evidence that every role has been redesigned effectively or that outcomes are equitable without ongoing measurement.23

Use job design and sourcing practices to operationalize the model

Job postings should focus on the work itself and state responsibilities as clear, action-oriented tasks, rather than relying mainly on credential filters.14 A realistic job preview can help candidates understand daily work, while employee quotes or testimonial videos can communicate workplace culture.14

Employers can broaden access by promoting telework and hybrid options, making postings screen-reader friendly, avoiding images that contain embedded text, and tracking outreach by skills-based criteria rather than traditional credentials.14 Maintaining candidate relationships through structured follow-up, virtual career information sessions, employee referrals, retiree networks, alumni networks, and professional associations can support sourcing beyond conventional pipelines.14

Skills-based screening should still distinguish meaningful credentials from weak signals. The evidence identifies several indicators of credential quality: substantial associated job opportunities; clearly defined competencies aligned with job skill requirements; evidence of improved employment and earnings outcomes; job-quality indicators such as benefits, sick leave, and dependable hours; stackability into further education or training; and portability across employers and job opportunities.15 Capable data systems are necessary to assess non-degree credential quality and connect credential information with skill needs.15

Evaluate selection outcomes, not merely intent

Adverse impact is an outcome measure: under the Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures, it is “a substantially different rate of selection” in hiring, promotion, or another employment decision that disadvantages members of a race, sex, or ethnic group.3 A process can create adverse impact without discriminatory intent; disparate treatment, by contrast, requires intentional discrimination.322 The Supreme Court recognized disparate-impact liability in *Griggs v. Duke Power Co.* in 1971, and Congress codified it in the Civil Rights Act of 1991.3

The four-fifths rule is a practical screening test: if a race, sex, or ethnic group’s selection rate is below 80% of the selection rate for the group with the highest rate, adverse impact may be present.722 The rule is a guideline rather than a conclusive legal threshold, and further statistical analysis may be necessary to determine significance.22

Employers should keep records that disclose the impact of tests and other selection procedures on employment opportunities by identifiable race, sex, and ethnic group.7 Where applicant volumes are large and procedures are frequent, appropriate and adequately sized samples may be retained.7 The Guidelines specify recordkeeping by sex and by listed race and ethnicity categories, while requiring safeguards so those records are used appropriately for impact analysis or, where required, affirmative-action monitoring.7

For hiring, annual impact determinations should be made for groups that make up at least 2% of the relevant labor force and for groups constituting more than 2% of applicants; recordkeeping obligations continue even for smaller groups.16 Records should be retained for people who were applicants or candidates at any stage of the selection process.16

The total selection process should be evaluated first. If the overall process shows adverse impact, individual components should then be evaluated; if the overall process does not show adverse impact, enforcement agencies will usually not require component-level evaluation, subject to specified exceptions.7 Those exceptions include procedures that perpetuate assignment patterns caused by prior discrimination and procedures, such as height or weight requirements or no-arrest-records rules, that courts or administrative interpretations have found not job-related in similar circumstances.7

Apply structured evaluation methods consistently

Behavioral interviews can use the STAR method, which structures evidence around Situation, Task, Action, and Result.18 For interviewers, STAR provides a consistent way to prompt complete, job-relevant answers and assess candidates against defined competencies instead of vague or subjective impressions.18

Interview answers should be scored with a rubric or scorecard that defines strong, average, and weak answers for each question.18 This supports more consistent evaluations across interviewers and reduces reliance on unstructured judgment.18

Monitor candidate experience alongside efficiency

Automated screening can move more filtering to the application stage before candidates interact with a recruiter or hiring manager, making the process more top-funnel heavy through higher application volume and more first-stage rejections.17 Candidate experience was reported as largely unchanged and, in some segments, increasingly fragile, with large organizations experiencing more dissatisfaction tied to impersonality and delays.17 Expectation setting was identified as the most consistent source of dissatisfaction.17

Measurement should therefore cover both access and experience: degree-free posting rates, skills-based outreach effectiveness, stage-by-stage selection rates, demographic impact, candidate withdrawal, time spent at each stage, and whether candidates receive clear expectations and communication.7

Reception

Candidate Experience and Perceptions

Candidate experience was largely unchanged in 2025 and became increasingly fragile in some segments; large organizations experienced more friction tied to impersonality and delays. Expectation setting was the most consistent source of dissatisfaction across the dataset.17

Across the full dataset, 37.8% of candidate comments mentioned expectation setting, and 74.5% of those comments were negative. Negative sentiment also appeared at every journey stage: 75.2% among rejected candidates who commented on expectation setting, 70.9% among withdrawn candidates, and 39.1% even among hired candidates.17

The reported sources of negative expectation-setting comments were:

| Theme | Share of negative expectation-setting comments |

|---|---:|

| Lack of clarity or transparency | 24.

| Timeline issues or delays | 9.

| Misleading information compared with the job description | 4.

Candidates specifically reported missing information about what was being assessed, what success looked like, when they would hear back, and why they were rejected.17

More than half of all AI-related candidate comments, 56%, carried negative sentiment. Among rejected candidates, 22% referenced AI or automated filtering in their comments. Rejected and withdrawn candidates who mentioned AI were predominantly negative, while hired candidates mentioning it were overwhelmingly positive.17

The evidence attributes this split less to automation itself than to a lack of transparency about how decisions are made. Candidates who advanced described automation as efficient and organized; candidates rejected early without explanation described it as impersonal, lazy, or unfair.17

Candidates increasingly form their view of an employer at the application stage, before they interact with a recruiter or hiring manager. As early filtering expands, a rejection email may define the candidate experience and shape employer-brand perceptions.17

Employer and Hiring-Manager Adoption

Research on managers’ interview practices found a consistent preference for unstructured interviews over highly structured interviews, even when managers understood the intended structured format. Managers’ intentions were shaped most by personal attitudes toward each method and perceived workplace norms; only intentions favoring unstructured interviews aligned with actual behavior. The researchers concluded that attitudes and local culture, rather than lack of know-how, are major barriers to adopting structured interviewing.18

Structured interviews use pre-defined lead and follow-up questions for every applicant and score responses against proficiency benchmarks. Higher interview structure is associated with greater validity, rater reliability, rater agreement, and lower adverse impact.24

Despite these advantages, applicants and interviewers tend to prefer less-structured interview formats, although interviews generally produce more favorable applicant reactions than other common selection measures.24

Public and Policy Reception

Skills-based hiring has drawn bipartisan support in the United States, with state and federal governments moving to remove unnecessary degree requirements from job postings.22

More than half of U.S. states have issued policy directives focused on skills-based hiring. In 25 states and one territory, those policies directed state hiring entities to reassess job requirements and prioritize skills, work history, and credentials rather than simply requiring a degree.2

Analysis of state commitments found increased public awareness of skills-based concepts such as the “paper ceiling,” defined as the bachelor’s-degree analogue of the glass ceiling. In states with commitments, bachelor’s-degree requirements in state-government job postings fell by an estimated 2.5 percentage points for each additional year of policy exposure.23

However, employer implementation has not consistently matched public commitments. A Harvard Business School and Burning Glass Institute study of more than 11,000 U.S. job postings from 2014 through 2023 found that hiring of candidates without degrees increased by 3.5 percentage points in jobs where degree requirements were removed. Because those jobs represented a small share of all hiring, the estimated overall effect was less than one percentage point, with fewer than one in 700 new hires benefiting from no-degree reforms.6

Skills-based hiring: definition, adoption, and process design

Skills-based hiring assesses candidates against demonstrated ability to perform a role’s essential functions, specific competencies, and observable skills, rather than relying mainly on degrees, job titles, employer brands, or prior experience.11089 Skills-first and skills-based approaches are related but distinct: skills-first is a broader organization-wide approach, while skills-based hiring concerns the recruiting and selection process.12

Reported adoption rates vary by study and population. NACE found that 64.8% of surveyed employers used skills-based hiring for new entry-level hires, and more than half used it always or most of the time.10 A separate 2024 global report placed overall adoption at 81% and found that 95% of employers agreed skills-based hiring would be the dominant recruitment method of the future.13 Another source reported that 85% of employers use skills-based hiring practices, while 64.8% apply them to entry-level hiring.9 Kelly reported that 70% of employers used skills-based hiring for entry-level roles in early 2026, up from 65% the prior year.12

Use is concentrated at later funnel stages: 90% of employers using skills-based hiring apply it during interviews, and about two-thirds use it during screening.10 NACE’s Job Outlook 2026 survey of 183 employers reported similar results, with 87% applying the approach at interview and 65% during screening.5 Nearly three-quarters of employers in the NACE study had developed competency-based job descriptions.10

Process consistency determines whether the model changes outcomes. Kelly recommends that sourcers, recruiters, and final interviewers use the same rubric; it argues that changing job descriptions without changing sourcing and evaluation procedures leaves the old system intact.12 Its recommended sequence is to pilot a few measurable roles, map competencies from top performers, train hiring teams, and update job descriptions last.12 Time-to-fill and retention are identified as the two core evaluation metrics, although both require consistent implementation before the resulting data can be interpreted.12

Degree-screening changes provide related evidence. The share of job postings with no formal education requirement rose from 48% in 2019 to 52% in early 2024, while postings requiring a bachelor’s degree or higher fell by 2.6 percentage points.21 One source states that 45% of organizations removed bachelor’s-degree requirements for some roles in 2024, on top of 55% that had already done so in 2023; it also cites a poll of 800 U.S. employers in which about 80% preferred relevant experience to a college graduate credential.11

Structured interviews and competency-based selection

Structured interviews use job-relevant questions, predefined scoring criteria, and standardized administration to assess candidates consistently.24 The source describes strong content validity because interview situations closely represent job conditions, criterion-related validity because structured-interview performance relates highly to job performance, and construct validity through moderate relationships with cognitive ability and personality measures.24 Structured interviews can also add incremental validity beyond cognitive-ability and personality measures.24

Interviews usually produce more favorable applicant reactions than other common selection methods, although both applicants and interviewers tend to prefer less structured formats.24 Structured interviews may be conducted individually in person, by telephone, or through video conference.24 Faking occurs less often in structured interviews than in paper-and-pencil inventories assessing the same competencies.24

The Canada Research Chairs EDI guide recommends providing search committees with a detailed method for writing job descriptions that identify required skills, abilities, experience, and qualities; advice for evaluating Indigenous ways of knowing, community-based research, and non-peer-reviewed publications; suggested and impermissible interview questions; accommodation considerations; and steps that make decision-making open and transparent.25 The guide identifies intersectionality, a term Kimberlé Crenshaw coined in 1989, as necessary for understanding barriers faced by people who belong to more than one underrepresented group.25

Pre-employment assessments and testing technology

Pre-employment assessments include cognitive-aptitude, personality, skill, integrity, emotional-intelligence, situational-judgment, and role-specific tests used before hiring decisions.2619 Cognitive aptitude tests accounted for the largest reported share of the global pre-employment assessment-tools market in 2025, at 31.4% of revenue or about $1.51 billion.26 A separate pre-employment testing-software market estimate for 2024 instead placed skill assessments first at 34% ($0.65 billion), followed by cognitive ability tests at 26% ($0.49 billion), personality and behavioral tests at 24% ($0.46 billion), and integrity and situational-judgment tests at 16% ($0.30 billion).19

The global pre-employment assessment-tools market was estimated at $4.8 billion in 2025 and projected to reach $10.9 billion by 2034, a 9.5% CAGR for 2026-2034.26 North America held 38.2% of reported 2025 revenue, and HireVue, SHL, and The Predictive Index were identified as leading vendors.26 A different estimate valued pre-employment testing software at $1.9 billion in 2024 and projected $3.2 billion by 2030, an 8.1% CAGR.19

Cloud deployment represented 72% ($1.37 billion) of the 2024 pre-employment testing-software estimate, compared with 28% ($0.53 billion) for on-premises deployment.19 Large enterprises accounted for 63% ($1.20 billion), while small and medium enterprises represented 37% ($0.70 billion).19 IT and telecom led end-user demand at 24% ($0.46 billion), followed by BFSI at 18% ($0.34 billion) and healthcare at 15% ($0.29 billion).19

Assessment vendors are incorporating gamified testing and virtual simulations to make evaluations more engaging and to give employers more information on candidates’ skills and competencies.27 One market source claims comprehensive pre-employment assessment investment is associated with a 30% decrease in turnover.27 Another reports that automated scoring, candidate ranking, and structured assessment libraries reduce recruiter screening workload by an estimated 22.6% and improve shortlisting throughput by 31.4% in high-volume hiring.19

Several claimed outcome figures should be treated as source-specific benchmarks: one assessment-market source states that structured tools can improve quality of hire by up to 36% and reduce turnover costs by an average of 22%.26 Another source states that structured pre-employment testing can reduce turnover by up to 25% in high-volume retail, logistics, and customer-service industries.19 Vendor and employer case-study data cited in a recruiting-statistics report indicate that pre-hire assessments can reduce time-to-hire by up to 50% by filtering mismatched applicants earlier.5

Quality of hire, retention, and credentials

A source on skills-based talent acquisition reports that 75% of employers using skills-based assessment tools saw improved quality of hire and that adopters tended to report about 30% higher employee retention within two years of hire.9 A separate statistics compilation cites McKinsey research stating that skills-based hiring is five times more predictive of job performance than education-based hiring and more than twice as effective as work experience alone.5

Credential quality remains relevant when employers reduce degree requirements. The National Skills Coalition describes a quality credential as one connected to substantial job opportunities; grounded in clearly defined competencies aligned with job requirements; associated with improved employment and earnings outcomes; linked to job-quality indicators such as health or retirement benefits, sick leave, and dependable hours; stackable into additional education or training; and portable across employers and job opportunities.15 The same source argues that states need capable data systems to judge non-degree credential quality and make credential quality and skill needs easier to identify.15

Fairness, adverse impact, and candidate trust

Skills-based processes can reduce reliance on proxy signals, but selection tools still require validation and demographic-impact monitoring.919 The Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures provide the federal framework for evaluating tests and other selection procedures, particularly discrimination risk and validation expectations.19 Software that includes validation documentation, cut-score governance, and demographic-impact monitoring was estimated by one market report to reduce manual compliance-review exposure by 18.9%.19

The EEOC received 88,531 new discrimination charges in fiscal year 2024, a 9.2% increase from the prior year, and recovered nearly $700 million for more than 21,000 victims.3 Under the EEOC’s Uniform Guidelines, adverse impact concerns a substantially different selection rate in hiring, promotion, or another employment decision across demographic groups.3 The Canada Research Chairs guide defines equity as removing systemic barriers and biases so all individuals have equal access to and can benefit from programs, requiring institutions to identify and address barriers such as racism and ableism in policies and work environments.25

Candidate confidence is a separate constraint on AI-enabled screening. Gartner reported in 2025 that only 26% of job applicants trusted AI to evaluate them fairly.28 Employers adopting AI-supported assessment should therefore pair automation with transparent evaluation criteria, documented validation, accommodation practices, and monitoring for adverse impact.25

Publication History

- HR Acuity launched its first Employee Relations Benchmark Study in 2016 using calendar-year 2015 data. The organization describes the study as having evolved from a focus on case response, policy interpretation, and localized practice into a more structured, data-enabled employee-relations benchmark connected to workplace risk, trust, and issue prevention.29

- NACE’s *Job Outlook 2025* was published in November 2024. Its Class of 2025 hiring projections were collected from NACE employer members between August 5 and September 16, 2024; the subsequent *Job Outlook 2025 Spring Update* refreshed those projections and also received 40 responses from nonmember companies.30

- NACE has tracked employers’ use of GPA as a new-graduate screening criterion for several years: nearly three-quarters of employers reported using GPA filters in 2019, the share fell to 37% in 2023, and *Job Outlook 2025* reported a rebound to 46%.10

- A cited skills-based-hiring benchmark from TestGorilla reported that 81% of employers used skills-based hiring in 2024, compared with 56% in 2022; 90% of adopting employers reported better diversity outcomes.3
