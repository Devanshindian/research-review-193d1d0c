How it runs, in short

You do not want the technical detail, and you do not need it to judge the output. Here is the whole idea in a few lines.

One command, same result every time
A single command runs the whole thing. It works through five steps in a fixed order, and it can be put on a schedule so it runs on its own. It does not improvise: give it the same topic and you get the same shape of output every run. That is what makes it safe to trust and repeat across dozens of topics.

The one step worth a mention
For the deep evidence (step 2) we use a method that works like a small newsroom. It invents a few expert interviewers, each one interviews a researcher who may only answer from web pages it reads in that moment, and then it writes up what they found with a source behind every line. Nothing comes from memory, so every fact traces back to a real page. If you want to see exactly how any step works, just ask and I will walk you through it.

The topic we researched

Testlify State of Skills-Based Hiring 2026: an annual benchmark report

Our angle: an annual report that stays fully free, covering the numbers most vendors keep behind a form. Assessment drop-off, time spent in a test, candidate satisfaction, AI cheating, and quality of hire by screening method. The closest competitor gates theirs; ours is open, and refreshed each year. That open, free position is the thing we can win on.

1Keyword and competitor research

What it is
The search terms worth targeting, who ranks for them today, and the gap we can win.
Why it matters
It points the article at demand we can realistically rank for, and names the opening no competitor has taken.
Main term
skills based hiring. About 880 searches a month, and low difficulty to rank (12 out of 100). Winnable.
The opening we can own
No page that ranks today publishes a free annual benchmark of assessment-stage numbers, AI cheating, candidate experience, and quality of hire by method. That is our whole angle.

Supporting terms it found (all low difficulty)

Each of these can anchor its own section, and most are easy to rank for: predictive validity, cut score, construct validity, content validity, criterion validity, disparate impact, employee turnover rate, 2026 recruitment.

Who ranks now

A mix of HR bodies, media, and vendors: NACE, SHRM, AIHR, Workday, Robert Walters, the U.S. Chamber Foundation, Handshake, and TestGorilla. Strong names, but none of them offers the free annual benchmark we are planning.

It also plans ahead
The same step surfaced a set of follow-up article ideas (we call them spokes) that this report could later link out to, each scored and ranked so only the best few get picked up. Those become their own articles down the line.

3The gap-check

What it is
A check that the evidence actually covers everything the article needs, with a top-up when it does not.
Why it matters
It stops a thin spot from reaching the writer. If something important is missing, the engine goes back and researches it before moving on.

Five more were only partly covered. The engine ranks the misses by how much they matter, then runs a small number of targeted research rounds to close the important ones. It does not chase every tiny gap, which keeps it focused.
