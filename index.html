Where this fits

A quick recap so this sits in the right place. Earlier we shared the shortlisted content ideas as a reviewable list, and Aparna and Aakash looked those over. That page is still live here: the clubbed ideas review.

Those ideas did not come out of thin air. We found them three ways, each aimed at a different kind of link-worthy asset:

  • Studying competitors: we looked at the pages that already earn competitors the most backlinks, and took the winning format to build our own version on a topic we own.
  • Modelling other niches: we borrowed proven asset formats from other industries, like calculators, indexes, and awards, that no competitor in our space has built yet.
  • Tracking trends: we read what the field is actively talking about, for example on Reddit, and shaped the recurring themes into timely assets.

This is the phase that comes after. We take one idea from that list and the engine researches it from start to finish, then hands a writer a ready-to-write pack. Below is the real output for one of those ideas, our 2026 skills-based hiring benchmark report. Nothing here was written by hand. It is what the engine produced.

How it runs, in short

One command, same result every time
A single command runs the whole thing. It works through five steps in a fixed order. It is deterministic: give it the same topic and you get the same shape of output every run. It is also cron-able, so it can be set to run automatically on a schedule with no one driving it. That is what makes it safe to trust and repeat across dozens of topics.

Part two: the research it did for this topic
9Keyword and competitor research›

The starting point. What it does:

  • Builds the full list of search terms around the topic, and grades each one by how many people search it and how hard it is to rank for.
  • Reads the live Google results to see who ranks now and what kind of pages win.
  • Reads the top competitor pages to find what they all cover, and the gap none of them fills.
  • Pulls the "People also ask" questions and the AI answer box, so we know what readers and AI expect.
  • Scores every term for how well it fits our angle, so we chase demand we can actually win, not just big numbers.

Cost: this is the one step that pays for live search data (the DataForSEO API), roughly 50 cents to a dollar for the whole topic. Everything else in the pipeline runs on our own AI for free.

Key results for this topic
  • Main keyword: skills based hiring (about 880 searches a month, low difficulty at 12 out of 100).
  • Supporting terms, each its own section: predictive validity, cut score, construct validity, content validity, criterion validity, disparate impact, employee turnover rate, 2026 recruitment.
  • Variations woven in: skill based hiring, skills based recruitment, skill-based recruiting, and a few more.
  • The opening we can own: no ranking page offers a free annual benchmark of assessment-stage numbers, AI cheating, candidate experience, and quality of hire by method.
  • Who ranks now: NACE, SHRM, AIHR, Workday, and TestGorilla, among other HR bodies and vendors.

10The evidence pack›

The deep-research step. It works like a small newsroom:

  • It invents a few "editors," each with a different angle, so the topic gets covered from several sides at once.
  • Each editor interviews an expert that may only answer from web pages it reads in that moment.
  • It searches, opens the real pages, and reads the full articles, not just the search snippet.
  • Every answer is written with a citation, and every source is kept.
  • It then writes all of that up into one long, fully-sourced document, with a source behind every line.

Cost: the AI work runs on our own subscription for free. The only small cost is the web-search lookups.

Key results for this topic
  • 7,042 words of researched, cited notes.
  • 30 real sources, one behind every claim.
  • 3 extra targeted rounds run to fill gaps the first pass missed.
  • Sample fact it pulled: NACE's Job Outlook 2026 survey of 183 employers found 70% used skills-based hiring, up from 65% the year before.

11The gap-check›

A check on its own work. How it works:

  • It audits the evidence against the keyword brief, one item at a time.
  • For each thing the article must cover, it asks whether the evidence has the substance to write it, and answers covered, partly, or not, quoting a real line as proof.
  • For the important misses, it writes a few sharp new research questions and runs the evidence step again to fill them.
  • It caps those top-ups at two or three, and ranks the misses by importance, so it fills what counts and skips trivia.
Key results for this topic
  • 30 must-cover items checked: 19 fully covered, 5 partly, 6 missing.
  • Ran 3 extra targeted research rounds to close the important misses.
  • The original evidence pack was left untouched; the top-ups are kept alongside it.
See the full gap-check ↗
12The article blueprintthe main output›

Where it all comes together, and the most important piece of the bundle. How it is built:

  • It breaks every source into small cards, one idea each.
  • It scores each card against our angle and drops the off-topic ones, so the article stays on point.
  • It groups the surviving cards into sections, so each idea sits in exactly one place, and names each section.
  • Each section gets its own target keyword, chosen for low difficulty and healthy search volume, so more of the article can rank, not just the top of it.
  • It also pulls in Testlify's own relevant pages as internal links under each section. That gives the article ready-made links back to our existing content, which strengthens both the research and our SEO.
  • It attaches the supporting evidence and the outside sources to each section too, ready to cite.
  • The result is a section-by-section plan the writer opens and writes from, with nothing left to hunt for.
Key results for this topic
  • 20 possible sections (a menu; the writer trims to the four to seven that ship).
  • 76 sub-points, with about 1,700 pieces of evidence attached.
  • 5 sections flagged as our own angle, the parts competitors do not cover.
  • 274 off-topic pieces filtered out before the plan was built.
