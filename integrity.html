Writing integrity

Writing Integrity — Testlify (the honest + distinctive writing contract)

The rules for making truthful, well-sourced, distinctive claims when writing an article. Not the SEO/AEO/GEO
checklist (structure/format) — this is about honesty, positioning, sourcing, and standing out. Read by /write.

1. Authority honesty — claim only what Testlify actually is

Position Testlify only where it genuinely wins. When the topic is broad ("recruiting", "hiring", "talent
strategy"), write the broad context honestly, then locate Testlify at the assessment/screening slice. Claiming
one slice well beats claiming the whole stack vaguely.

  • Testlify IS: a pre-hire talent-assessment platform — skills tests, role-based assessments, cognitive ability, personality, coding/technical assessments, a large test library, anti-cheat/proctoring, and the candidate experience around screening.
  • Testlify is NOT (never imply it owns these): an ATS, a sourcing/CRM tool, an HRIS/payroll system, background checks, onboarding, or a video-interview suite. We integrate with parts of the stack; we don't replace it.

2. Honesty tell — say when we're NOT the right fit

State plainly where Testlify isn't the answer (e.g. very small teams, a niche need). It builds trust and the
comparison pages already do this. Don't pretend to fit everyone.

3. Don't overpromise; acknowledge limitations

No guaranteed outcomes, no "this fixes hiring." Acknowledge trade-offs and limits. Transparent > salesy.

4. No empty hype / unprovable superlatives

Never claim big and prove nothing. Ban "the ultimate / game-changing / revolutionary / seamless / world-class"
with no number behind it. No buzzword soup ("synergistic, holistically, paradigm"). Every strong claim carries a
real, cited number or it's cut.

5. Differentiation + illustrative stories — say something only Testlify would say

The piece must carry a real point of view, a non-obvious trade-off, or a concrete workflow — not generic vendor
filler. Clearly-hypothetical scenarios are allowed ("a 500-person SaaS company hiring 20 engineers a quarter
could screen with a coding assessment before the first call…") tied to a genuine Testlify capability.

  • HARD BAN: no named or implied real customer · no fake quote · no fabricated first-party metric (win rate, time-to-hire delta, NPS, customer count). Use real Testlify first-party data ONLY if it's actually supplied for the run.

6. Self-consistency — the numbers must reconcile

List every number used and confirm none contradict each other (a maturity %, an adoption %, an ROI figure, a
time-to-hire must all be possible together). PASS/FAIL; fix the conflict (re-verify or drop the stat) before shipping.

7. Never cite a competitor

Never cite or link a direct competitor as a source (list: asset-engine/competitor-study/output/competitors.md;
also auto-enforced upstream by the research-structure filter). If the only source for a stat is a competitor,
drop the stat or use a neutral primary. Critique competitor approaches generically, never by name.

8. Cite with credibility

Draft from any source, but when you place an external citation, prefer the most credible + most recent
source available — judge credibility on the merits; there is no fixed whitelist.

9. Cite, don't promote

A citation is evidence for one stat, not an endorsement. Never promote or drive traffic to another brand's
content. State the fact, cite the source (neutral, nofollow), move on. ≤2 citations per external domain; aim 3+ distinct domains.

10. No stat quota — use stats where they earn their place

Don't force a fixed number of statistics, and don't stuff. Use a stat where it genuinely supports the point; a few
load-bearing verified stats beat many impressive-sounding ones. Natural over volume.
